A Translated Menu to Order Food in an Informed and Confident Way

After moving to the US, I noticed that most restaurant menus are text-only — no photos, no visual cues. Coming from a culture where visual menus are the norm, I quickly realised how disorienting it is to order without knowing what a dish actually looks like. That personal frustration became the starting point for this project.

Problem Statement

"What even is this dish?"

When international diners can't read or understand menu text, the entire ordering experience breaks down. Without visual references to bridge the language gap, they're left guessing — turning what should be a relaxing cultural experience into one filled with anxiety and decision fatigue.

Goal

The goal is to speed up their decision-making process and enable them to order with confidence. Transforming the anxious experience into a fast, clear, and easy one.

Discover

I surveyed international diners to validate the problem firsthand — mapping how they currently navigate unfamiliar menus and quantifying the emotional friction in the ordering experience.

Based on primary and secondary research, users will benefit from a single tool that replaces the multi-app workflow. It would reduce order anxiety by allowing users to translate the text into their familiar language. The experience would become much easier with dish images as visual reference, dietary and ingredients context, and tax & tip explanation.

Competitive Analysis

What's already out there

Competitive analysis benchmarks existing solutions to understand what the market already does well and where it falls short. By mapping features, accessibility, and user flows across key players, I was able to identify unmet needs and whitespace that informed the product direction. This analysis ensured the design would offer something meaningfully different — not just another translation tool.

Competitive Analysis

+ Instant overlay that replaces on the physical menu − No contextual description − Lacks currency conversion

Gap

No existing tool combines image-first menu translation, dietary and flavor context, preference-based menu highlights, and tipping guidance in a single flow — leaving international diners to piece together information across multiple apps at the moment they need clarity most.

Learnings

What I learned

💡

AI tools are helpful, but a designer's judgement is essential.

Throughout the project, I used AI tools to review survey questions, brainstorm feature opportunities, and explore visual directions. They work best as a thought partner, but understanding the full context, connecting all the pieces, and making the right call is still the designer's job.

💡

Business thinking needs to be part of the process from the start.

I grounded decisions in business context throughout by understanding what metrics actually signal and how design choices connect to real outcomes. Good design doesn't just solve user problems; it has to serve the business too.
